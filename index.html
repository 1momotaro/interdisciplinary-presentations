Structuring a Strong Opening

4 / 13

Why opening structure matters

A mixed disciplinary audience arrives with different backgrounds and expectations. If you open with specialist content before establishing common ground, you risk losing large parts of your audience in the first two minutes.

In Japanese academic contexts, a clear and respectful opening typically moves through five stages:

1
Introduce yourself briefly
A brief introduction to your name, institution, and field helps the audience contextualise your perspective from the outset.
2
Establish shared common ground
Start with something every discipline can relate to — a broad question or a shared challenge. This creates belonging before difference.
3
Hook with a real-world problem
Create relevance and motivation before introducing your research. The audience needs a reason to care about what you are about to say.
4
State what your research addresses
Now that they are engaged, focus their attention on your specific contribution. Keep this clear and jargon-free.
5
Preview the structure
Tell the audience what is coming. This reduces cognitive load and helps non-specialists navigate the presentation.

What Do You Notice?

8 / 13

Observe these two moments from Yuki's presentation. For each one, select the observation that resonates most with you — every choice reveals a useful insight.

Moment A — Delivery

"As can be seen in the data displayed on this slide, the results clearly indicate that..." Several audience members begin checking their phones.

What do you notice here?

All three observations point to something real. The most significant issue is loss of eye contact — when a presenter reads from the screen, the connection with the audience breaks immediately. Eye contact signals engagement, confidence, and respect. Knowing your content well enough to face your audience is one of the most impactful delivery skills you can develop.

Moment B — Data Presentation

"So here's the data." Yuki advances to a complex graph, pauses, then moves to the next slide. Audience members exchange confused glances.

What would have helped here?

Each of these could help in different contexts. The most universally effective strategy is to frame the data before revealing it — tell the audience exactly what question to bring to the graph, and what the key finding is. This works whether the graph is simple or complex, and it dramatically reduces cognitive load for non-specialists.
